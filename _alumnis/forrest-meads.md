**From EMT to Leadership: Forrest’s Path Through Emergency Care**

Before enrolling at Kankakee Community College, Forrest Meads was a certified nursing assistant at a nursing home in the Kankakee area.

“I saw EMTs coming in to pick up patients for dialysis,” he said.

This resonated with Forrest, who has had Type 1 diabetes for most of his life. The condition  requires constant management and has long-term risks, even with careful attention.

“I thought what they were doing was important,” he said. “When I talked to them, they told me they all went to KCC to get their EMT training.”

Motivated by those conversations, Forrest enrolled at KCC and completed EMT-Basic training in 2016 before continuing into the paramedic program.

“The first time I tried to go through the paramedic program, I kind of emptied my pockets,” he said. “Everything hit at once.”

Advanced coursework, clinical demands, and full-time work collided with the realities of managing Type 1 diabetes.

“It’s like a ticking time bomb on a seesaw,” he said. “You’re trying to keep it balanced, but it can tip at any time. You can be following your diet, taking your insulin, doing everything right, and then you get sick anyway.”

After stepping back, Forrest returned to the paramedic program. This time, he was awarded a scholarship from the Women’s Giving Circle, a program that brings together community-minded women to support KCC students facing financial barriers. The scholarship allowed him to focus on completing the program.

“That scholarship helped me finish,” he said. “It helped me get the license that changed the direction of my life. They don’t treat you like a kid. They hold you responsible, but if you show effort and want to finish, they’ll help you get there.”

Forrest earned his paramedic credential in 2018 and passed his licensing exam on the first attempt.

Forrest credits paramedic instructor Susan Mosier–“Mama Sue” to students–with helping him through moments of doubt.

“Even when I didn’t think I could pass a test or get through a clinical, she’d say, ‘No, I’m getting you through this class,’” he said.

He also recalled frequent check-ins with the dean of health sciences at the time.

“I was in her office at least once a week,” Forrest said. “They kept an eye on me.”

Forrest went on to work as a paramedic for five years, including one year responding to 911 calls in Sauk Village during the COVID-19 pandemic.

“You see people on the worst days of their lives,” he said. “Sometimes you’re the last person someone sees.”

The intensity of the work, combined with the realities of managing a chronic condition, led Forrest to move away from frontline emergency response. For three years, he worked in the Safety and Health Department of Amazon in University Park.

“It was a way to keep using my medical background without destroying my body,” he said.

In that role, Forrest applied his emergency training, clinical judgment, and risk-assessment skills in a corporate setting. While his career has shifted, he views his education at KCC as foundational.

“It gave me a foot in the door,” he said. “Not just in medicine, but in understanding systems and people.”

For students considering KCC, Forrest emphasized preparation and using available support.

“The programs give you what you need,” he said. “What matters is knowing your limits and using the support that’s there.”
